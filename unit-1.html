ONE NATION, TWO SYSTEMS, FORTY YEARS

1949 – 1989 · The Greatest Economic Experiment of the 20th Century
Learning Targets: The 3 Key Questions Factors of Production Command vs. Free Market Economic Goals & Trade-offs Price Makers vs. Price Takers Mixed Economy
"What if you could run the same country as two completely different economic experiments — at the same time?"

In 1945, Germany lay in ruins. The Allied powers divided it into occupation zones, and by 1949 two separate nations had formed from the rubble — same language, same culture, same people, many with family on the other side of the wire. For 40 years, the two Germanys answered the three key questions of economics in almost perfectly opposite ways. The results were dramatic, measurable, and impossible to ignore.

Fig. 1 — Children walk alongside the Berlin Wall, Berlin, c. 1962. Built overnight in August 1961, the Wall stretched 96 miles through the city. The GDR called it the "Anti-Fascist Protection Rampart." Everyone else called it a prison wall. Over 140 people were killed trying to cross it.
Section 01 · Background
Part 1 — Germany Partitioned
How One Country Became Two

World War II ended in May 1945 with Germany in rubble. The four Allied powers — the United States, Britain, France, and the Soviet Union — each occupied a zone. The plan was temporary. It wasn't.

By 1949, two completely separate nations had crystallized from the occupation zones. The Western zones became the Federal Republic of Germany (West Germany) — a parliamentary democracy backed by the U.S. and NATO. The Soviet zone became the German Democratic Republic (East Germany) — a communist state modeled on the USSR, despite the word "democratic" in the name.

Families were split. Cities were divided. Berlin, sitting entirely within East German territory, was itself cut in two. In 1961, East Germany built the Wall — not to keep Westerners out, but to stop its own people from leaving. More than 3.5 million East Germans had fled West since 1949, and the economy was hemorrhaging its workforce. The Wall was an admission of failure dressed up as a security measure.

It stood for 28 years. When it fell on November 9, 1989, crowds tore it down with hammers within hours. Germany officially reunified on October 3, 1990.

Part 2 — The Perfect Economic Experiment
Why Economists Still Talk About This

Comparing economic systems is usually messy. Different countries have different cultures, resources, climates, histories. How do you isolate the effect of the economic system itself?

Germany is the closest thing economists have to a controlled experiment. The starting conditions in 1949 were as close to identical as history ever produces: same people, same language, same destroyed infrastructure, same industrial base, same culture, even the same trauma. The only variable — the economic system — was then changed completely and deliberately.

West Germany adopted free-market capitalism with a strong welfare safety net (the Soziale Marktwirtschaft). East Germany adopted a Soviet-style command economy where the state owned and controlled virtually everything.

Run that experiment for 40 years, then open the border and see what happens. By 1989, West Germans were roughly three times wealthier by GDP per capita. East Germans flooded West — and abandoned everything they'd owned in the East, including their cars. The experiment had a clear result.

"Prosperity for all."
— Ludwig Erhard, West Germany's first Economics Minister, 1957
Section 02 · The Two Systems
How Each System Answered the 3 Key Questions
East Germany · Command Economy
Central Planners Decide Everything

In East Germany, a central planning agency answered all three key questions for the entire economy — for every factory, farm, and household in the country.

What to make? Planners set production targets for every factory: how many apartments, shoes, and cars. The goal was to meet basic needs equally.

How to make it? The state owned all factories, land, and equipment — the factors of production. Workers were assigned jobs. Factory managers chased quotas, not profit.

Who gets it? The state distributed goods based on need. Housing, healthcare, and education were provided to all. Wages were relatively equal across professions.

"We will overtake West Germany within ten years."
— Walter Ulbricht, East German Leader, 1958
West Germany · Social Market Economy
Markets Decide, Government Protects

West Germany adopted what its architects called the Soziale Marktwirtschaft — free markets answer most questions, but the government provides a safety net and sets the rules.

What to make? Businesses and entrepreneurs decided based on consumer demand and the profit motive. If customers wanted it and someone could profit from making it, it got made.

How to make it? Private firms owned the factories and equipment. Competition forced efficient, innovative methods. Volkswagen, BMW, and Mercedes competed furiously for every customer.

Who gets it? Consumers buy what they can afford. The government provides unemployment insurance, healthcare, and pensions — but doesn't control distribution of most goods.

"The market delivers what central planners can only promise."
— Economic maxim of the Wirtschaftswunder era

When East Germans could finally access Western cars, they abandoned Trabants in parking lots by the thousands. Worthless on the open market. This is what happens when consumer choice is restored after decades of no alternatives — and what it reveals about which system people actually preferred.

The Trabant was designed by state engineers in the late 1950s. Because there was no competition and no profit motive, there was no incentive to improve it. Factory managers hit their production quotas — that was "success." The car still used a two-stroke engine and a plastic body made of compressed wool and resin when production ended in 1991. It hadn't meaningfully changed in 30 years.

Meanwhile in the West, Volkswagen, BMW, Mercedes, Porsche, and Audi competed for every customer. Each year brought new features, better fuel economy, improved safety. This is what happens when businesses are price takers in a competitive market — innovation isn't optional, it's survival. In the East, the Trabant factory was a price maker with no rivals and captive customers. Stagnation was guaranteed.

The Trade-offs Are Real — Don't Let Anyone Tell You Otherwise

Look at the scorecard above. East Germany scores better than West Germany on employment, equity, and welfare. West Germany wins on growth, efficiency, and freedom. This is the core trade-off of the Unit 1 framework playing out in real life.

Discussion: If you had to live in one of these countries in 1975 — not knowing anything about their future — which would you choose, and why? Does your answer change if you're a factory worker? A doctor? A single parent? What does that tell you about who economic goals actually serve?

Extension: West Germany's system is called a "social market economy" — not pure capitalism. Where did it keep the government involved? Why? Does that complicate the "free market vs. government" framing?

Two Germanys, One Framework — Apply What You Know
  • In East Germany, who answered the three key questions — and what did they use to make those decisions? In West Germany, who answered them — and what drove their choices?
  • The Trabant went virtually unchanged for 30 years. Use the concepts of price makers, price takers, and competition to explain why. What would have had to change for East Germany to produce a better car?
  • East Germany had essentially zero unemployment. Is that automatically a good thing? What does it tell us about the trade-offs between economic goals?
  • West Germany's system is called a "social market economy" — not a pure free market. What does that suggest about whether the real choice is ever truly "government vs. free market"?
  • Synthesis: When the Wall fell, East Germans flooded West Germany and abandoned their Trabants. In economic terms, what were they revealing about which goals they personally prioritized? Does a population "voting with their feet" tell us something markets and ballots can't?
Glossary
Key Terms for This Case Study
Command Economy
A system where the government answers the three key questions — deciding what gets produced, how, and who receives it. Also called a planned economy.
Free Market Economy
A system where individuals and businesses make production and consumption decisions through voluntary exchange, guided by supply and demand rather than government direction.
Mixed Economy
A system combining free market and government elements. Both Germanys were mixed — no pure system exists in practice.
Factors of Production
Land, Labor, Capital, and Entrepreneurship — the inputs needed to produce goods and services. A key question: who owns and controls these?
Price Taker
A firm in a competitive market that must accept market price — it has no power to set its own. Forced to compete on quality and efficiency to survive.
Price Maker
A firm with enough market power to set its own price. In East Germany, the state set all prices. In monopolistic markets, a single firm can set prices without competitive pressure.
Economic Trade-offs
Gains in one economic goal often come at the cost of another. East Germany achieved equity but sacrificed efficiency. West Germany achieved growth but accepted inequality. There is no free lunch.
Profit Motive
The incentive to earn profit drives firms in market economies to innovate and respond to consumers. Its absence in command economies is a key reason for stagnation — and the Trabant problem.
