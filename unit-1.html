ONE NATION, TWO SYSTEMS, FORTY YEARS

1949 – 1989 · The Greatest Economic Experiment of the 20th Century
Learning Targets: The 3 Key Questions Factors of Production Command vs. Free Market Economic Goals & Trade-offs Price Makers vs. Price Takers Mixed Economy
"What if you could run the same country as two completely different economic experiments — at the same time?"

In 1945, Germany lay in ruins. The Allied powers divided it into occupation zones, and by 1949 two separate nations had formed from the rubble. West Germany became a capitalist democracy aligned with the United States. East Germany became a communist state aligned with the Soviet Union. Same language. Same culture. Same history. Same people — many with family on the other side of the border. For 40 years, the two Germanys answered the three key questions of economics in almost perfectly opposite ways. The results were dramatic, measurable, and impossible to ignore.

Section 01
The Setup: Why This Is the Perfect Economic Experiment

Most of the time when economists compare economic systems, they're comparing different countries — but those countries have different cultures, different resources, different histories, different languages. It's hard to isolate the effect of the economic system itself.

Germany is different. The division of Germany in 1949 was essentially a controlled experiment: take one nation, split it down the middle, and run two opposing economic systems simultaneously. The starting conditions were as close to identical as history ever produces. By 1989, the differences were staggering.

West Germany (FRG) launches as a parliamentary democracy with a market economy. East Germany (GDR) launches as a communist state under Soviet influence.
1961
The Wall Goes Up
East Germany builds the Berlin Wall to stop mass emigration. Over 3.5 million East Germans had fled West since 1949 — the Wall was an admission that people were choosing with their feet.
1989

East Germans flood checkpoints after the GDR announces it will open the border. Within hours, crowds are tearing down the Wall with hammers. The experiment ends.
1990

Germany officially reunites. Western economists arriving in the East discover factories using 1950s technology, a currency worth nearly nothing, and an economy that will take decades to rebuild.

East Germany · Section 02
How the Command Economy Answered the 3 Questions

In East Germany, the government — through a central planning agency called Gosplan — answered all three key questions for the entire economy.

What to make? Central planners set production targets for every factory. They decided how many apartments to build, how many pairs of shoes to produce, how many cars. Their goal was to meet the basic needs of all citizens equally.

How to make it? The state owned all the factories, land, and major equipment — the factors of production. Workers were assigned to jobs. Factory managers had quotas to meet, not profits to chase.

Who gets it? The state distributed goods based on need and political priorities. Housing, healthcare, and education were provided to all. Wages were relatively equal across professions.

"We will overtake West Germany within ten years."
— Walter Ulbricht, East German Leader, 1958

West Germany · Section 02
How the Market Economy Answered the 3 Questions

West Germany adopted what its architects called the Soziale Marktwirtschaft — the "Social Market Economy." It was capitalism with a strong welfare state: free markets answer most questions, but the government provides a safety net.

What to make? Individual businesses and entrepreneurs decided what to produce based on consumer demand and the profit motive. If consumers wanted it and someone could make money selling it, it got made.

How to make it? Private firms owned the factories and equipment. Competition forced them to find efficient, innovative methods. Automakers like Volkswagen, BMW, and Mercedes-Benz competed aggressively for customers.

Who gets it? Consumers buy what they can afford. The government provides a safety net — unemployment insurance, healthcare, pensions — but doesn't control distribution of most goods.

"Prosperity for all."
— Ludwig Erhard, West Germany's first Economics Minister, 1957

The Trabant was designed by state engineers in the late 1950s. Because there was no competition and no profit motive, there was no incentive to improve it. Factory managers hit their production quotas — that was success. The Trabant still used a two-stroke engine and a plastic body made from compressed wool fiber and resin when production ended in 1991. When the Wall fell and East Germans could finally access Western cars, they abandoned their Trabants in parking lots by the thousands. The cars were worthless on the open market.

Meanwhile in the West, Volkswagen, BMW, Mercedes-Benz, Porsche, and Audi competed furiously for every customer. Each year brought new features, better fuel economy, improved safety. Price competition kept costs relatively accessible. This is what happens when businesses are price takers competing for survival rather than price makers with captive customers and no rivals. Innovation isn't a nice-to-have — it's the difference between staying in business and going under.

The Trade-offs Are Real — Don't Let Anyone Tell You Otherwise

Look at the scorecard above. East Germany scores better than West Germany on employment, equity, and welfare. West Germany wins on growth, efficiency, and freedom. This is the core trade-off of your Unit 1 framework playing out in real life.

Discussion questions: If you had to live in one of these two countries in 1975 — not knowing anything about their future — which would you choose, and why? What if you were a factory worker? A doctor? A single parent? Does your answer change? What does that tell you about the relationship between economic goals and the people they serve?

Extension: West Germany's system is called a "social market economy" — it's not pure free market capitalism. Where did West Germany keep the government involved? Why? Does that change how you think about the "free market vs. government" framing?

Check for Understanding
Two Germanys, One Framework — Apply What You Know
  • In East Germany, who answered the three key questions of economics — and what did they use to make those decisions? In West Germany, who answered them — and what drove their decisions?
  • The Trabant went virtually unchanged for 30 years. Use the concepts of price makers, price takers, and competition to explain why. What would have had to change for East Germany to produce a better car?
  • East Germany had essentially zero unemployment. Is that automatically a good thing? What does it tell us about the trade-offs between economic goals?
  • West Germany's system is called a "social market economy" — not a pure free market. What does that suggest about whether the real choice is ever truly "government vs. free market" — or something more complicated?
  • Synthesis: When the Wall fell, East Germans flooded West Germany — and abandoned their Trabants. In economic terms, what were they revealing about which goals they personally prioritized? Does a population "voting with their feet" tell us something markets and ballots can't?
Glossary
Key Terms for This Case Study
Command Economy
A system where the government or central planners answer the three key questions — deciding what gets produced, how, and who receives it. Also called a planned economy.
Free Market Economy
A system where individuals and businesses make production and consumption decisions through voluntary exchange, guided by supply and demand rather than government direction.
Mixed Economy
A system combining free market and government elements. Both Germanys were mixed — the West had welfare programs; the East had some small private commerce. No pure system exists in practice.
Factors of Production
Land, Labor, Capital, and Entrepreneurship — the inputs needed to produce goods and services. A key question of economic systems: who owns and controls these?
Price Taker
A firm in a competitive market that must accept market price — it has no power to set its own. Forced to compete on quality and efficiency to survive.
Price Maker
A firm with enough market power to set its own price. In East Germany, the state set all prices. In monopolistic markets, a single firm can set prices without competitive pressure.
Economic Trade-offs
Gains in one economic goal often come at the cost of another. East Germany achieved equity but sacrificed efficiency. West Germany achieved growth but accepted inequality. There is no free lunch.
Profit Motive
The incentive to earn profit drives firms in market economies to innovate, cut costs, and respond to consumers. Its absence in command economies is a key reason for stagnation and the Trabant problem.
