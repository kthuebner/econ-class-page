Two Market Failures at Work
🔒
Market Failure #1
Information
Asymmetry
When one party in a transaction has significantly more information than the other. Free markets assume buyers and sellers have roughly equal information — when they don't, the market breaks.
How It Played Out
  • Purdue knew OxyContin was highly addictive. Doctors and patients didn't.
  • Patients consented to prescriptions based on false information — not a real free market transaction.
  • Doctors prescribed in good faith, unaware they were being misled by the company's own sales reps.
  • The consumer had no realistic way to discover the truth on their own — this is exactly when markets fail and regulation is needed.
📤
Market Failure #2
Negative
Externalities
When the production or sale of a good creates costs that are paid by people who had nothing to do with the transaction. The cost goes external.
Who Paid the Price
  • Families — addiction, broken households, children placed in foster care.
  • Communities — lost workers, rising crime, devastated small towns.
  • Taxpayers — emergency services, addiction treatment, incarceration costs.
  • The healthcare system — billions in treatment costs that Purdue never paid.
Primary Source · Marketing Material
"Believe in the Power"

This OxyContin advertisement ran in medical journals between 1997 and 2003, targeting doctors directly. The aspirational imagery and confident tagline — "start with it, stay with it, trust it" — reflects Purdue's aggressive sales strategy. At the time this ad ran, Purdue's own internal documents showed executives were aware of growing addiction reports. Doctors reading this ad had no access to that information. This is information asymmetry in action.

Pharmaceutical advertisement, OxyContin (oxycodone HCl), Napp Pharmaceuticals Ltd., c. 1999. Shown here for educational purposes as a primary source document illustrating deceptive marketing practices.

The transaction was between Purdue and a patient. But the real costs — addiction treatment, lost income, foster care, emergency services — were paid by people who never bought a single pill. This is the definition of a negative externality. The price of OxyContin never reflected its true cost to society.

Government Response · Fixing the Market Failure
Purdue Pleads Guilty · $8.3 Billion Settlement · 2020

In 2020, Purdue Pharma pleaded guilty to federal charges and agreed to an $8.3 billion settlement — the largest ever for a pharmaceutical company. The Sackler family, who owned Purdue, paid an additional $6 billion. The company dissolved. But for many economists and communities, the response came 25 years too late. The settlement represented a fraction of the estimated $1 trillion in social costs the crisis created. This raises the core question of Pigouvian economics: if the fine doesn't equal the damage, does it actually fix the market failure?

Check for Understanding
Questions
What Went Wrong — and Why?
  • In your own words: what is information asymmetry? How did it apply to the relationship between Purdue Pharma, doctors, and patients?
  • Purdue Pharma made roughly $35 billion from OxyContin. Who paid the other costs of the opioid crisis — and why didn't those costs show up in the price of the drug?
  • The free market is supposed to be good at efficiently allocating goods. Why did it fail here? Which of the two market failures — information asymmetry or negative externalities — do you think was more responsible?
  • Pigouvian thinking: If the opioid crisis cost society $1 trillion, but Purdue only paid $8.3 billion in fines, what does that tell you about whether the punishment "fixed" the market failure?
  • Apply it: Can you think of another industry where consumers might not have full information about the risks of a product? What should the government do about it?
  • Debate: Some argue the FDA, not just Purdue, bears responsibility for approving the drug. Does that change your analysis of the market failure? Who should have fixed it — and when?
