Speculative Markets · Case Studies

What Makes It
a Bubble?

A speculative bubble forms when the price of an asset, a stock, a tulip, a dot-com company, gets driven far above its actual value by excitement, hype, and the simple belief that someone else will pay even more tomorrow. Bubbles aren't caused by stupid people. They're caused by smart people who all start believing the same story at the same time.

Stage 01
Displacement

Something new and exciting arrives: a technology, a trade route, a commodity.

Stage 02
Boom

Prices risee and arly investors profit. Word spreads and more money floods in.

Stage 03
Euphoria

Number go up, everyone is 'getting rich' Skeptics are ignored.

Stage 04
Profit Taking

Insiders start quietly selling, making a profit. Late buyers start to panic.

Stage 05
Panic

Panic selling begins, prices collapse. The same asset everyone loved is now crashing.

Keep this cycle in mind as you read through history.

Dutch Republic · 1634–1637

The Tulip Craze

The world's first recorded speculative bubble

In the 1630s, the Dutch Republic was the wealthiest, most financially sophisticated nation on earth. They had invented the stock market, futures contracts, and joint-stock companies. When tulips arrived from the Ottoman Empire, they weren't just pretty flowers. They were exotic, scarce, and a powerful status symbol among the merchant class that had money to spend.

Buyers who got in during the 1620s made serious money as demand from wealthy collectors grew. A rare bulb bought for 100 guilders might sell for 400 two years later. As this continued, prices kept going up as more and more people jumped in hoping to make easy money buying and then selling at a higher price.

What made certain varieties so valuable was a strange quirk of biology: a mosaic virus that infected some bulbs created wild, unpredictable streaks of color that couldn't be reliably reproduced. The Semper Augustus, with its dramatic red and white flames, was genuinely one of a kind. The beauty was real but the price was disconnected from reality.

By 1636, the market had attracted a new kind of participant: tavern traders with no horticultural knowledge buying futures contracts on bulbs they'd never seen, in varieties they couldn't evaluate, expecting to flip them before delivery ever came. The people trading these assets no longer understood them.

In February 1637, a routine bulb auction in Haarlem failed to attract a single bid at the asking price. Word spread fast in the tight Dutch merchant community. Everyone holding bulbs was also expecting to sell them. When it became clear that the next buyer might not be coming, no one wanted to be the last person holding an asset in freefall. Prices collapsed within weeks.

The core economic lesson isn't that Dutch merchants were foolish. Each individual decision made some sense at the time: buy something scarce that wealthy people want, sell it to someone who wants it more. The collective mistake was assuming the chain of buyers would never end. That assumption is at the heart of every speculative bubble.

Interlude · Brief Historical Note

The Roaring Twenties

The 1920s were a genuine economic boom. WWI was over, factories were humming, and new technologies like the radio and the automobile were transforming daily life. Stock prices rose steadily through the decade, at first for good reason: American companies really were becoming more productive and profitable.

Soon, even the shoe-shine boys had hot stock tips. Seemingly everyone was getting rich, but the prices had become disconnected from the underlying value of companies. Investors weren't buying stocks because of earnings; they were buying because prices kept going up and they didn't want to miss out.

Investors could buy stocks with as little as 10% down, borrowing the rest on margin. This amplified gains on the way up and devastated investors on the way down. When confidence cracked in October 1929, margin calls forced mass selling, banks failed, and the Dow lost nearly 90% of its value by 1932. The Great Depression followed.

The key economics concept: leverage amplifies bubbles. Borrowed money makes the ride up steeper and the crash far more destructive. Keep that in mind as you read the next two sections.

By the year 2000 it was clear that the internet was going to change everything. The potential for new business ideas was enormous. As these new DotCom businesses popped up, investors didn't wait for actual profits to materialize before bidding valuations into the stratosphere. A company could go public with no revenue, a ".com" in its name, and a vague idea, then watch as its stock price tripled on day one.

The logic was simple: get big fast. Grab market share, get more users... figure out how to make money later. Money poured in, stock prices shot up, and Super Bowl ads were purchased by companies that had barely launched. When the Federal Reserve raised interest rates in 2000 and investor confidence cracked, the crash was swift. Companies with no path to profitability evaporated overnight.

The DotCom bubble can seem silly when you think about it as companies with no profit having skyrocketing stock prices and a Super Bowl ad, but some of these companies not only survived -- they became massive (Amazon and Google). Others collapsed but had decent ideas; the technology just wasn't there yet. Pets.com and Webvan tried to deliver pet food and groceries to your doorstep. Even the logic of growing first and finding revenue later turned out to be a winning strategy for companies like Facebook, Twitter, and other social media companies ten years later.

The point is this: a truly transformative technology can make it harder to see the bubble forming right in front of our eyes...

🤖 Is There an AI Bubble? 🫧

DATA CENTERS  /  GPU SHORTAGES  /  $TRILLIONS IN CAPEX  /  BORROWED MONEY

Since ChatGPT launched in late 2022, the world's largest technology companies have committed hundreds of billions of dollars to AI infrastructure: data centers, chips, power plants, and fiber. The scale of the spending is genuinely historic. And increasingly, they are borrowing to do it.

The spending is mind-boggling, but the revenue to justify it isn't there yet. Companies are betting that AI will eventually generate enough profit to pay back the loans and build something lasting. That logic should sound familiar after reading the previous two sections.

What This Diagram Shows

Notice that the money doesn't just flow one direction. Microsoft invests in OpenAI. OpenAI buys Nvidia chips. Nvidia invests back into OpenAI. Oracle takes a $300 billion cloud deal from OpenAI. Everyone is simultaneously a customer, a supplier, and an investor in everyone else.

This is called a circular investment loop. It can make a sector look healthy because money keeps moving. But it also means that if one major player hits trouble, the shock ripples outward to everyone connected. The dot-com era had a similar web of cross-investments. When confidence cracked, the whole web came down at once.

The question economists are asking: how much of this activity represents real new value being created, and how much is the same money flowing in circles?

The borrowing figures are striking. But several economists argue the deeper problem is the gap between what is being spent and what is actually coming back.

Only 3% of consumers currently pay for AI services, generating roughly $12 billion in annual revenue. The same companies building this infrastructure are committing $635 to $700 billion in spending in 2026 alone. A National Bureau of Economic Research study found that 90% of firms report no measurable impact of AI on workplace productivity. The technology is everywhere. The payoff, so far, is not.

Another concerning factor deals with the hardware. The Nvidia chips powering today's data centers cost roughly $30,000 each and are functionally obsolete within about a year as newer, more powerful models arrive. Unlike a building or a factory, which depreciates slowly over decades, the physical collateral backing billions in AI loans loses most of its value almost immediately. That is a risk that many lenders may not have fully priced in.

Then there is the competitive dynamics problem. Every major company is spending as if they will be the winner of the AI race. But not all of them can win. There is a finite amount of demand, and someone is going to end up with excess capacity. This is the same dynamic that buried the fiber optic telecom companies after the dot-com bust: they built more than the world needed, and when demand failed to materialize fast enough, the debt became unpayable.

Here is what makes this different from the dot-com bubble: Harvard economist Jason Furman estimates that AI-driven infrastructure investment accounted for 92% of U.S. GDP growth in the first half of 2025. The dot-com crash hurt investors and wiped out tech companies, but it also caused a mini-recession. An AI crash would be much worse. It would remove what is currently the primary engine of the entire U.S. economy. This bubble, if it is one, could take down the entire economy if it pops.

The Parallels -- You Decide

Revenue vs. Spending

Dot-com companies promised profits later. AI companies are committing $635–700 billion in 2026 alone while generating roughly $12 billion in actual consumer AI revenue. The debt is real. The returns are still projected.

FOMO-Driven Investment

In 1999, every company had to be a dot-com or get left behind. Today every major corporation claims to be an "AI company." No one can blink first. Fear of missing out drives decisions more than fundamentals.

One Supplier Captures All the Gains

Cisco made the routers everyone needed in 1999 and peaked at the top of the bubble. Nvidia makes the GPUs everyone needs today. Its stock rose over 800% since 2022 and has reached Cisco-like valuation territory.

The Infrastructure May Outlast the Hype

Fiber optic cables buried during the dot-com bust became the backbone of today's internet. The data centers going up now may prove equally foundational, even if current valuations collapse first. The technology can be real and the bubble can still pop.

  • 90% of firms report no productivity gains from AI yet
  • GPU chips worth $30K each are obsolete within a year
  • Borrowing accelerating far faster than any revenue to support it

  • "This time is different" as the main argument

Signs It's Real

  • Actual productivity gains measurable in specific sectors
  • Nvidia's revenue growth is real, not projected
  • Physical infrastructure retains value even after a crash
  • The buyers are the largest, most profitable companies in history
  • AI capabilities improving rapidly and demonstrably
  • Fiber built during the dot-com bust runs the internet today
