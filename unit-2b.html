Law of Demand

Higher Price = Less Demand

As the price of a good rises, consumers will demand less of it. As the price falls, they demand more. Price and quantity demanded move in opposite directions.

Law of Supply

Higher Price = More Supply

As the price of a good rises, producers want to make and sell more of it. Higher prices mean higher profit potential. Price and quantity supplied move in the same direction.

What Shifts Demand? T.O.N.I.E.

TTastes & Preferences of consumers OOther Goods (substitutes & complements) NNumber of Buyers in the market IIncome of consumers EExpectations about future prices

What Shifts Supply?

  • Number of sellers in the market
  • Technology used in production
  • Cost to produce the good (inputs, labor)
  • Government taxes, subsidies, or regulations
  • Price of other goods producers could make instead
  • Producer expectations about future prices

Price Elasticity of Demand

How sensitive are buyers when the price changes? PED measures exactly that.
The Formula
PED = % Change in Quantity Demanded % Change in Price
Worked Example: Insulin — A price increase of 50% leads to only a 5% drop in quantity demanded. People need insulin to survive, so they buy it almost regardless of price.
Step 1 — Price Change
Price rises from $200 to $300
% change = (300−200) / 200 × 100 = +50%
Step 2 — Quantity Change
Qty demanded falls from 100 to 95 units
% change = (95−100) / 100 × 100 = −5%
0.1

PED = −5% ÷ 50% = 0.1

We drop the negative sign and look at the absolute value. A PED of 0.1 is very close to zero, which means demand is highly inelastic. A big price change barely moved quantity demanded.

Inelastic Demand

Buyers are not very sensitive to price changes. Quantity demanded barely moves even when price spikes. Common for necessities with no substitutes.

PED < 1  |  Examples: insulin, gas, utilities

Elastic Demand

Buyers are very sensitive to price changes. A small price increase causes a large drop in quantity demanded. Common for luxury goods or products with lots of substitutes.

PED > 1  |  Examples: brand-name shoes, vacation travel, streaming services

Scenario 09  ·  Both Curves
Lumber
2021
MINI_CHART_9
COVID-19 hits. Lumber mills shut down and cut production (they expect low demand). At the same time, people stuck at home get stimulus checks and start building decks, fences, and home offices. Construction booms. Lumber prices quadruple.
Which curves? Which directions?
Supply shifts LEFT AND Demand shifts RIGHT simultaneously. Mills cut production based on expectations (a supply determinant), reducing supply. Stimulus income plus work-from-home tastes drove demand way up (income + tastes from T.O.N.I.E.). Both forces push the equilibrium price in the same direction: up.
Effect on Equilibrium
EQ Price rises dramatically. Quantity impact is ambiguous. When supply falls AND demand rises, prices are guaranteed to spike, but the net effect on quantity depends on which shift is larger. In 2021, prices roughly quadrupled. This is the hardest type of scenario because two things move at once.
