Section 1
The Free Market
Markets are incredibly powerful, but they are not perfect.

When Markets Work

A free market is an economic system where buyers and sellers make transactions freely. Prices are set by supply and demand. Producers make what they want, consumers buy what they want, and the government mostly stays out of the way.

Efficiency Innovation Growth Economic Freedom

When Markets Break

Leaving everything to the free market doesn't always produce a good outcome for society. Some things the market simply won't provide, and some situations the market actively makes worse. When this happens, we call it a market failure.

Public Goods Gap Monopoly Power Externalities Bad Information Overuse
Market Failure

A market failure happens when the free market does not allocate goods and services efficiently. In other words, the market does not produce outcomes that are optimal for society as a whole. The market "breaks" in some way, and the result is either something important not being provided, someone being harmed, or resources being wasted.

Section 2
Types of Market Failure
Click any category to expand the definition and examples.

A public good is something the public needs, but private businesses can't make profitable, so they won't produce it on their own.


Public goods have two defining characteristics:


Non-excludable: You can't stop people from using or benefiting from it, even if they didn't pay.


Non-rival: One person using it doesn't diminish it for anyone else.


If something is non-excludable, non-rival, and important, the government should generally provide it, because the free market won't.

Real Examples

Street lights: you can't exclude passersby, and one person using the light doesn't dim it for anyone else.
National defense: the military protects everyone whether or not they pay taxes. You can't let Russia only bomb the tax cheat's house.
Public roads and bridges: built for everyone, used by everyone.
Clean air and water standards: everyone benefits whether they contributed or not.

The Free Rider Problem

When people can benefit without paying, private firms have no way to profit, so no one builds it. People who benefit without contributing are called free riders.

The lifeblood of capitalism is competition. Businesses provide good products at low prices only because they have to compete. Competition also forces companies to innovate and stay efficient.


A monopoly is when one firm controls the entire market. Without competition, that firm can charge whatever it wants, produce less, and has little reason to improve.


Price makers set whatever price they want because there is no alternative for consumers. In a competitive market, firms are price takers and must follow supply and demand.


Natural Monopolies are a special case: some industries naturally work better as a single provider. Utilities like water, electricity, and natural gas are examples. Two companies laying separate pipes to every home makes no sense.

Real Examples

De Beers controlled roughly 80% of the world's diamond supply and could set prices far above what competition would allow.
Utility companies (electric, water, gas) are legal monopolies because competing infrastructure doesn't make sense.
Early Standard Oil controlled roughly 90% of U.S. oil refining. Congress eventually broke it up using antitrust laws.
Airline industry: an oligopoly where a few large firms control most routes, with significant barriers preventing new competitors from entering.

An externality is a cost or benefit caused by a producer that isn't paid for by the producer or consumer. The cost goes external, meaning it is paid by people who had nothing to do with the transaction.


Almost everything the government does is aimed at either fixing negative externalities or promoting positive ones. Many of the biggest problems society faces, including pollution, public health crises, and climate change, are caused by negative externalities.


The free market only works properly if all the real costs of producing something are factored into the price. When they aren't, prices are artificially low, leading to overconsumption and overproduction.

Negative Externalities

Factory pollution that wipes out a fishing industry: the fishers pay the cost, not the factory.
Burning fossil fuels: the cost of climate change and healthcare is not included in the price at the pump.
Fast fashion: cheap prices because environmental cleanup costs are externalized to society.

Positive Externalities

Vaccines: when you get vaccinated, you protect people around you who didn't pay for your shot.
Education: a more educated society benefits everyone, not just the student who got the degree.

Information asymmetry happens when one party in a transaction has significantly more or better information than the other. This breaks the free market's assumption that buyers and sellers are making informed decisions.


In a perfect free market, all parties have equal access to information. In reality, sellers almost always know more about the product than buyers, and this can be exploited.


When consumers can't evaluate what they're getting, they can't make good decisions. People get ripped off, or avoid buying something they actually need because they can't trust what's being sold.

Real Examples

Used car sales: the seller knows whether the car is a lemon; the buyer usually can't tell. Economist George Akerlof's "Market for Lemons" showed how this can collapse entire markets.
Healthcare: patients rarely know as much as doctors about what treatments they actually need, making it easy to be over-treated or misled.
Predatory loans: lenders design confusing terms that borrowers don't understand until it's too late.
FDA food labeling laws exist precisely to reduce information asymmetry between manufacturers and consumers.

The Tragedy of the Commons occurs when a shared resource gets used up because individuals, each acting in their own rational self-interest, overuse it, even though it destroys the resource for everyone in the long run.


The "commons" refers to any shared resource: fisheries, forests, clean air, groundwater. Each individual has an incentive to use as much as possible before others do. When everyone thinks this way, the resource collapses.


The free market alone can't solve this. There's no profit incentive to conserve something you don't own. Government regulation or clearly defined property rights are usually required to fix it.

Real Examples

Atlantic cod fisheries: individual fishermen had every incentive to catch as many fish as possible, leading to catastrophic population collapse in the 1990s.
Groundwater depletion: farmers pump as much as they can because there's no price signal stopping overuse of a shared aquifer.
Overfishing in international waters: no single country's regulations apply, so the resource gets overused by all.
Traffic congestion: every driver adds to everyone's commute time because the road is free to use with no limit.

Section 2C
Externalities
A cost or benefit that goes to people outside the transaction. The wrong people pay, or the wrong people benefit.
Negative Externality
Cost Goes External

When producing or consuming a good creates costs for third parties who had nothing to do with the transaction. The producer doesn't pay for the damage; someone else does.

Factory pollutes a river; fishermen lose their livelihood
Burning gas; healthcare and climate costs borne by society
Fast fashion; environmental cleanup not in the price tag
Cigarette smoke; bystanders bear health costs
Positive Externality
Benefit Goes External

When an individual's or firm's actions create benefits for others who didn't pay for them. The producer isn't compensated for the full benefit they create, so the market tends to underproduce.

Vaccines; herd immunity protects people who weren't vaccinated
Education; a more educated society benefits everyone
Research and innovation; discoveries benefit far beyond the inventor
Beekeeping; neighboring farms get free pollination

How Externalities Distort Markets
When costs go external, prices are artificially low, which means more is demanded and more is produced than is actually good for society.
Overconsumption: Because the price of gas doesn't include the costs of climate change and healthcare, the price at the pump is artificially low. Consumers demand more gas than they would if they were paying the true cost. The demand curve shifts right and quantity demanded goes up.
How do you fix a negative externality?

Pigouvian Tax

A tax placed directly on the negative externality, usually pollution. The goal is to make the producer pay the true cost of their output so it gets factored into the price. This uses market forces and the profit incentive to solve the problem efficiently, without an outright ban.

Regulation

The government simply bans or limits the negative externality. A hard cap on emissions, for example. The advantage is that it stops the harm from happening at all. The downside is that it can be less flexible than a tax-based approach.

Scenario Cards
Read the scenario. Identify the market failure. Flip the card to check your answer.
Scenario 01
A town's shared underground aquifer supplies all the local farms. Every farmer pumps as much water as possible because there is no price or limit on usage. The aquifer is depleting faster each year.
Market Failure Type
Tragedy of the Commons
A shared resource is overused by individuals each acting rationally in their own self-interest, depleting it for everyone.
Scenario 02
A pharmaceutical company is the only producer of a life-saving medication. There are no generic alternatives and no competing products. They charge $500 per pill.
Market Failure Type
Monopoly
With no competition, the firm is a price maker. Consumers have no alternatives, so the market fails to produce a fair outcome.
Scenario 03
A city wants to build new streetlights in a neighborhood. A private company declines: there is no way to charge people for walking under them at night.
Market Failure Type
Public Good
Streetlights are non-excludable and non-rival. No profit motive exists, so the government must step in to provide them.
Scenario 04
A coal plant operates near a small town. Children in the area have significantly higher asthma rates, but the cost of treating their illness isn't included in the price of the electricity the plant sells.
Market Failure Type
Negative Externality
Health costs are paid by the townspeople, not the coal plant. The cost went external. Prices are artificially low and the plant overproduces.
Scenario 05
A man buys a used car from a private seller. The seller knows the transmission is failing. The buyer has no way to find this out and pays full price. The car breaks down a week later.
Market Failure Type
Information Asymmetry
The seller has information the buyer doesn't. This is the classic "Market for Lemons" problem. When buyers can't tell quality from junk, the market breaks down.
Scenario 06
A city is considering free vaccinations for all residents. Without the program, many low-income residents won't get vaccinated, putting the broader community at risk even if they personally got the shot.
Market Failure Type
Positive Externality
Vaccines create benefits for people who didn't pay for them (herd immunity). The free market underproduces, so the government subsidizes them.
Scenario 07
Three airlines control 80% of domestic routes. When one raises baggage fees, the others do the same within days. Prices rarely drop even when jet fuel gets cheaper.
Market Failure Type
Oligopoly
With so few competitors, firms act like price makers and can informally coordinate pricing. Insufficient competition is the market failure.
Scenario 08
An ocean fishery is open to all boats with no catch limits. Every company expands its fleet to maximize its haul before competitors take the fish. Within a decade, the fish population collapses.
Market Failure Type
Tragedy of the Commons
Each firm acted rationally: maximize catch now. But collectively, they destroyed the shared resource. This is exactly what happened to Atlantic cod in the 1990s.
Scenario 09
A lender offers mortgages with a low teaser interest rate that resets sharply upward after two years. The terms are buried in dozens of pages of fine print most borrowers never read.
Market Failure Type
Information Asymmetry
The lender knows exactly what the loan terms mean. The borrower doesn't. This type of gap, deliberately exploited, played a major role in the 2008 financial crisis.
