Markets are powerful. The profit motive drives innovation, creates jobs, and raises living standards. But profit-seeking can also be short-sighted, and when it is, real people pay the price.

Core Concept
Who Does a Corporation Serve?

In 1970, economist Milton Friedman published a famous essay arguing that corporations have exactly one obligation: maximize profit for their shareholders. That idea reshaped American business. But is it complete?

Friedman's View (1970)

"The social responsibility of business is to increase its profits."

The Stakeholder View

"A corporation's decisions affect far more than its investors."

In 2005, private equity firms KKR and Bain Capital bought Toys "R" Us for $7.5 billion. Most of that purchase price was borrowed, and the debt was placed on Toys "R" Us itself, not on the firms that bought it. The company spent roughly $400 million a year just on interest payments. Two Companies, Two Outcomes
The Short Game vs. The Long Game

In the early 2000s, both Toys "R" Us and LEGO were struggling toy companies. One was taken over by investors focused on short-term returns. The other was family-owned and made long-term bets on its own product. The outcomes could not be more different.

Short-Term Extraction

Toys "R" Us

Bought in a leveraged buyout in 2005. Debt was loaded onto the company. Management fees flowed to investors. With no capital left to invest, the company could not compete with Amazon or modernize its stores.

When it mattered most, there was no money to adapt. The company was not defeated by the market; it was hollowed out before the fight even started.

❌ Bankrupt 2017 · 33,000 jobs lost
Long-Term Reinvestment

LEGO

Also nearly bankrupt in 2003, after chasing licensing deals and rapid expansion. But as a family-owned company, there was no hedge fund demanding a quick payout. Leadership pulled back, reinvested in designers and core products, and trusted the long-term vision.

The bet paid off. LEGO did not cut its way to survival; it built its way there.

✓ World's largest toy company today
The key difference was not luck or the market. It was the time horizon of the people making decisions, and who they felt accountable to.
Follow the Money
Where Does Corporate Profit Actually Go?

When a company makes money, it has a choice: extract it now, or invest it for later. That single decision shapes whether the company survives, whether workers get raises, and whether a community keeps its jobs.

The Boeing number: In the years before the 737 Max crashes, Boeing spent $43 billion on stock buybacks vs. $15 billion on research and development. That ratio is the extraction model in one statistic. The same logic played out at Toys "R" Us, where debt servicing and management fees consumed what should have been investment capital.
When It Gets Worse
Short-Termism at Its Most Extreme
✈️

Boeing: When Cost-Cutting Kills

You remember the Boeing 737 Max crashes of 2018 and 2019. 346 people died. Investigations traced the cause to a cost-cutting culture that had taken over Boeing after its 1997 merger with McDonnell Douglas, a company run by financial managers, not engineers.

In the years before the crashes, Boeing spent $43 billion on stock buybacks, returning cash to shareholders, compared to $15 billion on research and development. The board approved another $20 billion in buybacks just two months after the first crash.

Toys "R" Us cost 33,000 people their jobs. Boeing's version of the same logic cost 346 people their lives. The Friedman doctrine does not come with a natural stopping point.
