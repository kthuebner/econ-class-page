Section 01

Setting the Scene: The World Before the Embargo

In the early 1970s, oil was cheap, abundant, and taken for granted. The United States, Western Europe, and Japan had built entire economies — their suburbs, factories, and power grids — on the assumption that petroleum would cost roughly $3 a barrel forever. Cars were big. Highways were long. Nobody insulated their houses.

Then, in October 1973, everything changed. The Arab members of the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries (OPEC) announced they would stop selling oil to the United States and several other Western nations. Within months, the price of oil had quadrupled. The result was one of the most dramatic economic disruptions of the 20th century — and it introduced the world to a word that economists hoped they'd never have to use: stagflation.

"The Arabs have oil. We have their money. They have us where they want us."
— Overheard on a Washington trading floor, 1973
Oct. 6, 1973

The Yom Kippur War Begins

Egypt and Syria launch a surprise attack on Israel. The U.S. airlifts military supplies to Israel in response.

Oct. 17, 1973

OPEC Announces the Embargo

Arab oil ministers cut production and embargo the U.S., Portugal, Netherlands, and others who supported Israel.

Nov. – Dec., 1973

Gas Lines Appear Across America

Stations run out of fuel. Odd-even rationing begins. Speed limits are cut to 55 mph. Sunday driving bans are imposed.

Jan. 1974

Oil Hits $12 a Barrel

From $3 in October to $12 by January — a 400% increase in three months. Inflation surges. Growth collapses.

March 1974

Embargo Lifted — Damage Done

The embargo ends, but oil prices stay high. The era of cheap oil is over. The global economy is permanently changed.

Section 02

What Is a Cartel? (And Why Producers Love High Prices)

In a normal competitive market, if you raise your price, your customers just buy from someone else. You're a price taker — the market sets the price, and you accept it or lose business. This is how most markets work: wheat farmers, factory workers, small retailers.

But OPEC member nations figured out a way to beat this system. By coordinating with each other — agreeing to all produce less oil at the same time — they could force prices up and nobody could "shop around." This coordination is what makes a cartel.

Price Taker

A producer who must accept whatever price the market offers. If a wheat farmer tries to charge more than market price, buyers simply buy from another farmer. Individual sellers have no power over price. This describes most producers in competitive markets.

Price Maker

A producer who has enough market power to set (or heavily influence) price. Monopolies are the extreme case. A cartel creates price-maker power through coordination — acting like one giant monopolist even though many sellers exist.

Cartel

A group of producers who agree to coordinate output and pricing — essentially colluding to act as one. OPEC members agreed to cut production together so no single member lost market share. The goal: keep prices artificially high and share the profits.

Why Do Producers Love High Prices?
More Revenue per Unit If you pump 1 million barrels a day, the difference between $3/barrel and $12/barrel is $9 million per day. OPEC nations made far more money selling less oil at higher prices.
Inelastic Demand Oil is hard to substitute quickly. Consumers and industries need it. Even at 4× the price, demand doesn't drop by 75%. This means raising prices dramatically increases total revenue.
Political Leverage High prices also gave OPEC members geopolitical power — the ability to punish countries they disagreed with (like the U.S. supporting Israel) by threatening their energy supply.

Section 03

How Oil Prices Drive Inflation

When oil quadrupled in price, it didn't just make driving more expensive. Oil is an input in almost everything — gasoline to move trucks, fuel to heat factories, petrochemicals to make plastics. When the cost of a critical input skyrockets, the cost of making everything rises. This creates a specific kind of inflation economists call cost-push inflation.

Demand-Pull Inflation

"Too much money chasing too few goods." The economy is overheating — consumers are spending freely, businesses can't keep up with demand, so prices rise. The economy is growing. This is inflation policymakers are used to managing: raise interest rates, cool spending.

Think: Post-WWII boom, tech bubble 1990s, COVID reopening 2021.

Cost-Push Inflation ← 1973

A supply shock drives up input costs, and businesses pass those costs to consumers. Output falls (businesses can't afford to produce as much), unemployment rises, AND prices rise simultaneously. This is the dangerous kind.

Think: 1973 oil embargo, 1979 Iran oil shock, COVID supply chain crunch.

Section 04

The Nightmare Scenario: Stagflation

Before 1973, economists largely believed inflation and unemployment were opposites — if one went up, the other went down. This relationship (called the Phillips Curve) gave policymakers a menu of choices: accept some inflation for lower unemployment, or vice versa.

The oil crisis broke this model completely. Suddenly, the economy had rising prices AND rising unemployment at the same time. The word economists invented for this impossible-seeming combination was stagflation — economic stagnation (slow growth, high unemployment) plus inflation.

Why was this so hard to fight? The usual cures were self-defeating. To fight inflation: raise interest rates → but that makes unemployment worse. To fight unemployment: lower rates, increase spending → but that makes inflation worse. The Federal Reserve was essentially trying to fight a fire with gasoline.

The Policymaker's Dilemma — 2×2 Economic States

↓ Inflation + ↓ Unemployment

The Goldilocks Economy. Low prices, people working. Every government's dream. Hard to achieve, hard to sustain. U.S. experienced this in the mid-1990s.

↑ Inflation + ↓ Unemployment

Classic demand-pull overheating. Economy growing too fast. Fix: raise interest rates to cool spending. Painful but manageable.

↓ Inflation + ↑ Unemployment

Recession with deflation risk. Fix: cut rates, stimulus spending. Standard Keynesian response. Also manageable.

↑ Inflation + ↑ Unemployment

STAGFLATION — 1974–75 U.S. Cures for one problem worsen the other. No good options. Caused severe political crisis. Required years of painful policy under Volcker to escape.

The U.S. didn't fully escape stagflation until Federal Reserve Chairman Paul Volcker raised interest rates to a shocking 20% in 1980–81 — essentially choking the economy on purpose to kill inflation. It worked, but triggered the worst recession since the Great Depression, with unemployment hitting 10.8%.

The lesson: cost-push inflation caused by a supply shock is far more dangerous and harder to treat than ordinary demand-pull inflation. It exposed the limits of the economic tools governments thought they had mastered.

Glossary

Key Terms Summary

Vocabulary for This Case Study
Cartel A group of producers who collude to fix prices or control supply, acting collectively as a price maker.
Price Taker A producer in a competitive market who must accept the prevailing market price with no individual power to influence it.
Price Maker A producer with enough market power to set or significantly influence the price of their good or service.
Demand-Pull Inflation Inflation caused by excessive demand — consumers have more money and bid up prices. Usually occurs during strong economic growth.
Cost-Push Inflation Inflation caused by rising input costs (like oil) that force producers to charge more. Can occur even when the economy is weak.
Supply Shock A sudden, unexpected disruption to the supply of a critical commodity, causing rapid price changes throughout the economy.
Stagflation A simultaneous combination of economic stagnation (high unemployment, slow/negative growth) and high inflation. Considered nearly impossible before 1973.
Phillips Curve The pre-1973 economic model suggesting a predictable trade-off between inflation and unemployment. The oil crisis revealed its limitations.
