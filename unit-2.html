Why Did
Lumber Prices
Quadruple?

The pandemic lumber price spike is one of the clearest real-world examples of supply and demand in action, ever.

Key Concepts: Supply Shifts Demand Shifts Determinants of Supply Determinants of Demand Equilibrium Price Qty Demanded & Supplied Elasticity (preview) Cost-Push Inflation Demand-Pull Inflation

In early 2020, a 1,000-board-foot package of lumber cost around $350. By May 2021, just 14 months later, that same package cost $1,700. Homebuilders were stunned. Deck projects became unaffordable overnight. Sawmills couldn't keep up.

This wasn't magic. It wasn't price gouging. It was supply and demand doing exactly what supply and demand does, just faster and more dramatically than usual. Two things hit at once: supply collapsed and demand exploded.

What Happened: The Full Picture
COVID-19 HIT LUMBER FROM BOTH SIDES AT ONCE
📉 SUPPLY SIDE
Cause 1 · Cost of Production
Sawmills shut down or cut hours. Panicked by early COVID forecasts, lumber companies expected a housing crash and pulled back hard.
Determinant: Expectations of producers
Cause 2 · Number of Sellers
Some mills closed permanently. Workers stayed home. The number of active producers in the market shrank rapidly.
Determinant: Number of sellers
Cause 3 · Input Costs
Trucking and transport costs spiked as supply chains broke down, making it more expensive to get lumber from mill to market.
Determinant: Cost to produce / transport

📈 DEMAND SIDE
Cause 1 · Income
Stimulus checks and unemployment benefits put money in people's pockets when there wasn't much to spend it on.
Determinant: Income (T.O.N.I.E.)
Cause 2 · Tastes & Preferences
Stuck at home, Americans started home improvement projects, including decks, fences, and additions, at a historic rate.
Determinant: Tastes (T.O.N.I.E.)
Cause 3 · Number of Buyers
Remote work triggered a suburban housing boom. More people wanted new homes, more contractors needed lumber.
Determinant: Number of buyers (T.O.N.I.E.)
DEMAND INCREASES ↗
Whole curve shifts RIGHT
The Result: New Equilibrium Price
Supply ↓ + Demand ↑ = Price 🚀
When supply falls AND demand rises at the same time, prices don't just go up. They can explode. Both forces push price in the same direction.

What Happened Next: The Market Responds
Core Concept · How Markets Self-Correct
Sky-High Prices Triggered Their Own Cure
Here's the thing about high prices in a free market: they send a signal. That signal starts a chain reaction that eventually brings prices back down. By late 2021 and into 2022, the lumber market started correcting, exactly the way economic theory predicts.
1
Quantity Demanded Falls
Buyers Pull Back at $1,700
At the new sky-high price, many homeowners scrapped or delayed their deck projects. Contractors started substituting cheaper materials where possible. The quantity demanded dropped: not because people wanted lumber less, but because the price was now too high for many buyers to justify.
Law of Demand: QD ↓ as price ↑
2
Quantity Supplied Rises
Producers Love High Prices and Respond
$1,700 lumber was incredibly profitable. Sawmills that had shut down reopened. Existing mills ran extra shifts and ramped up output. New investment flowed into production. Producers responded to the high price signal by supplying a much higher quantity, exactly what the supply curve predicts.
Law of Supply: QS ↑ as price ↑
3
Supply Curve Shifts Back
Conditions Normalize Over Time
As pandemic restrictions eased, supply chains recovered and transport costs fell. The COVID-era home improvement craze cooled as people returned to offices and restaurants. Stimulus money ran out. The whole supply curve gradually shifted back right, and the demand curve cooled, pushing the equilibrium price back toward normal.
Determinants normalize → curves shift
Bottom Line
By mid-2022, lumber prices had fallen back below $600, still higher than pre-pandemic but a massive drop from the peak. The market corrected itself. High prices attracted more supply and discouraged demand until a new, lower equilibrium was reached. This self-correcting mechanism is one of the free market's most powerful features.
💡
Vocab Preview · Elasticity of Demand
Not everyone responded to high lumber prices the same way. A homeowner building a backyard deck could easily postpone or cancel: their demand was elastic (price-sensitive). A contractor mid-project had no choice but to keep buying regardless of price: their demand was inelastic (price-insensitive). Same product, very different responses to the same price increase. We'll dig into elasticity more in a later lesson.
Connecting to the Bigger Picture
Preview · Unit 5
Lumber Prices → Inflation
When a major input like lumber gets more expensive, it ripples through the whole economy. There are two ways this creates inflation, and lumber did both.
Cost-Push Inflation
Rising input costs force producers to charge more. The supply shock pushes prices up from the production side.
🪙 Lumber version: Sawmills paid more for transport and labor, charged more, homebuilders passed costs to buyers, new home prices rose, construction inflation hit the whole economy.
Demand-Pull Inflation
Surging consumer demand bids prices up. Too many dollars chasing too little supply.
🪙 Lumber version: Stimulus money + home improvement boom = everyone wanted lumber at once, prices got bid up, contractors had to charge clients more, housing inflation spread.
