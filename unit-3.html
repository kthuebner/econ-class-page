When Money
Becomes Worthless

Germany 1921–1923 is the most extreme example of hyperinflation in modern history, and the clearest illustration of what money actually is and what happens when people stop believing in it.

Key Concepts: Hyperinflation vs. Inflation Why Printing Money Fails Expectations & Inflation Velocity of Money MV = PQ Trust & Fiat Money
Background — After losing World War I, Germany was forced to pay enormous war reparations to France and Britain. The government didn't have the money, so it did something that seemed like a shortcut: it printed more of it. At first this sort of worked. But as more marks flooded the economy, prices started rising. So the government printed more to keep up. Prices rose faster. So they printed even more. By 1923, the spiral had become one of the most catastrophic economic collapses in history.

Inflation vs. Hyperinflation
Regular inflation is 2–4% per year. Hyperinflation is 50%+ per month. Same word, completely different animal.
Dig deeper ▼
The Full Picture
Regular inflation means prices rising slowly over time. The Fed actually targets mild inflation of around 2% per year because it keeps money moving and encourages spending and investment. A little inflation means people have incentive to spend and invest now rather than hold cash forever.

Hyperinflation is a different animal entirely. The technical definition is prices rising more than 50% per month. At that rate, something that costs $1.00 today costs $1.50 next month, $2.25 the month after, and over $130 a year from now. The math compounds fast.
🇩🇪 Weimar at the peak: Prices were doubling every 3–4 days. Workers demanded morning pay because by afternoon their wages bought less than at noon. Money had completely stopped working as a store of value. People would rather have a wheelbarrow full of bread than a wheelbarrow full of cash.
🖨️
Why You Can't Print Your Way Out
More bills in circulation doesn't mean more real wealth. It just means the same wealth is split across more pieces of paper.
Dig deeper ▼
The Equation of Exchange
Economists use a simple equation to show why printing money causes inflation:
M × V = P × Q
M = money supply  ·  V = velocity  ·  P = price level  ·  Q = real output
Think of the right side (P × Q) as the total value of everything produced and sold in the economy. The left side (M × V) is the total money circulating to buy those things. The two sides must stay equal.

If the government doubles M (prints more money) but Q (real goods produced) stays the same, then P (prices) must double. You haven't created more stuff. You've just created more paper to buy the same stuff.
🇩🇪 Weimar version: Germany's factories weren't suddenly more productive. The same loaves of bread were being baked. But there were trillions more marks chasing them. So the price of bread didn't just go up, it went up by a factor of 800 million.

What Happened Next — Breaking the Spiral
How Hyperinflation Ends
Germany Killed the Mark and Started Over
The Rentenmark Fix
In November 1923, Germany replaced the mark entirely. 1 Rentenmark = 1 trillion old marks. A hard cap was placed on how many could ever be printed, ending the open-ended money creation that had caused the crisis.
Why It Actually Worked
The Rentenmark wasn't backed by gold. It was backed by a credible promise not to keep printing. People believed it would hold its value, expectations flipped, panic spending stopped, and velocity fell back to normal. The doom loop ran in reverse.
Key Takeaway
The Rentenmark worked not because of what it was made of, but because people believed in it. This is the whole foundation of fiat money: money has value because we collectively agree it does. Destroy that agreement and no amount of printing can save it.
Connecting to the Bigger Picture
Preview · Monetary Policy
Why Doesn't This Happen in the USA?
Two words: the Fed. The United States has an independent central bank whose whole job is to manage the money supply so we never end up in a Weimar situation. Germany in 1923 had no such institution. The government printed money whenever it needed to pay bills, with no one empowered to say no. The Federal Reserve can raise interest rates and slow the money supply even when the government doesn't want it to, and it has decades of credibility built up doing exactly that. When the Fed says it will keep inflation near 2%, people believe it, and as Weimar showed us, belief is literally everything. A central bank without credibility is just a printing press with a fancier name.
