Unit 3 Case Study · What Is Money?

When Money
Becomes Worthless

Germany 1921–1923 is the most extreme example of hyperinflation in modern history, and the clearest illustration of what money actually is and what happens when people stop believing in it.

Key Concepts: Hyperinflation vs. Inflation Why Printing Money Fails Expectations & Inflation Velocity of Money MV = PQ Trust & Fiat Money
After losing World War I, Germany was forced to sign the Treaty of Versailles in 1919 and pay enormous war reparations to France and Britain. The German government didn't have the money, so it did something that seemed like a shortcut: it printed more of it. At first this worked, sort of. But as more marks flooded into the economy, prices started rising. So the government printed more to keep up. Prices rose faster. So they printed even more. By 1923, the spiral had become one of the most catastrophic economic collapses in history.

In January 1922, a loaf of bread in Germany cost 250 marks. A year later it was 700 marks. By November 1923, ten months after that, the same loaf cost 200 billion marks. Workers were paid twice a day so they could spend their wages before prices rose again. People burned banknotes for heat because it was cheaper than firewood.

This wasn't a supply chain problem. It was a government that had destroyed its own currency, mark by mark, until the money in people's pockets was worth less than the paper it was printed on.

Two Core Concepts
📈
Inflation vs. Hyperinflation
Regular inflation means prices rising slowly, around 2–4% per year. The Fed actually targets mild inflation because it keeps money moving and encourages spending and investment.

Hyperinflation is a different animal entirely: by definition, prices rising more than 50% per month.
🇩🇪 Weimar: At the peak in 1923, prices were doubling every 3–4 days. Workers demanded to be paid in the morning, because by afternoon their wages bought less than at noon. Money had stopped functioning as a store of value entirely.
🖨️
Why You Can't Print Your Way Out
Germany printed marks to pay its reparations. More bills sounds like more money, but it isn't more real wealth. Same amount of bread, same factories, just more pieces of paper chasing them.
M × V = P × Q
M = money supply  ·  V = velocity  ·  P = prices  ·  Q = real output
The math: If you double M but Q (real goods) stays the same, P (prices) must double. You haven't made anyone richer. You've just made every mark worth half as much... and then people figure that out.
50,000,000,000 mark note, issued 1923. Worth almost nothing by the time it was printed.

What Happened Next — Breaking the Spiral
How Hyperinflation Ends + Preview · Monetary Policy
Germany Killed the Mark and Started Over
You can't fix hyperinflation by printing a little less. Trust in the currency is already gone. In November 1923, Germany replaced the mark with a brand-new currency: the Rentenmark. Here's why it worked when nothing else had.
1
New Currency
The Rentenmark Replaces the Mark
1 Rentenmark = 1 trillion old marks. The old currency was declared worthless. A hard cap was placed on how many Rentenmarks could ever be printed, no more open-ended money creation to pay bills.
Money supply strictly capped
2
The Trust Fix
Credibility, Not Gold, Stopped It
The Rentenmark wasn't backed by gold. It was backed by a credible promise that Germany would not keep printing. People believed it would hold its value. Expectations flipped from inflationary to stable, and the doom loop ran in reverse.
Expectations anchor → spiral breaks
3
Stability Returns
Velocity Dropped. Prices Stabilized.
Once people trusted prices would stop rising, they stopped panic-spending. Velocity fell. In MV=PQ, V ↓ meant P stabilized. Within months the crisis was over... until the Great Depression hit in 1929.
V ↓ → P stabilizes
Key Takeaway
The Rentenmark worked not because of what it was made of, but because people believed in it. This is the whole foundation of fiat money: money has value because we collectively agree it does. Destroy that agreement and no amount of printing can save it.
Preview · Monetary Policy
Why Doesn't This Happen in the USA?
Two Words: The Fed
The United States has an independent central bank called the Federal Reserve, whose whole job is to manage the money supply so we never end up in a Weimar situation. Germany in 1923 had no such institution. The government printed money whenever it needed to pay bills, with no one empowered to say no. The Fed can raise interest rates, slow down the money supply, and take politically unpopular actions even when the government doesn't want it to. More importantly, it has decades of credibility built up, so when it says it will keep inflation near 2%, people believe it. And as Weimar showed us, belief is literally everything.
