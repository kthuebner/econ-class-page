Unit 3 Case Study · What Is Money?

When Money
Becomes Worthless

Germany 1921–1923 is the most extreme example of hyperinflation in modern history, and the clearest illustration of what money actually is and what happens when people stop believing in it.

Key Concepts: Hyperinflation vs. Inflation Why Printing Money Fails Expectations & Inflation Velocity of Money MV = PQ Trust & Fiat Money
After losing World War I, Germany was forced to pay enormous war reparations to France and Britain. The government didn't have the money, so it did something that seemed like a shortcut: it printed more of it. At first this sort of worked. But as more marks flooded the economy, prices started rising. So the government printed more to keep up. Prices rose faster. So they printed even more. By 1923, the spiral had become one of the most catastrophic economic collapses in history.

In January 1922, a loaf of bread cost 250 marks. A year later, 700 marks. By November 1923, the same loaf cost 200 billion marks. Workers were paid twice a day so they could spend their wages before prices rose again. People burned banknotes for heat because it was cheaper than firewood.

Two Core Concepts
📈
Inflation vs. Hyperinflation
Regular inflation means prices rising slowly, around 2–4% per year. The Fed actually targets mild inflation because it keeps money moving and encourages investment.

Hyperinflation is a different animal entirely: by definition, prices rising more than 50% per month.
🇩🇪 Weimar: At the peak in 1923, prices were doubling every 3–4 days. Workers demanded morning pay because by afternoon their wages bought less. Money had stopped working as a store of value entirely.
🖨️
Why You Can't Print Your Way Out
Germany printed marks to pay its reparations. More bills sounds like more money, but it isn't more real wealth. Same bread, same factories, just more pieces of paper chasing them.
M × V = P × Q
M = money supply  ·  V = velocity  ·  P = prices  ·  Q = real output
The math: If you double M but Q (real goods) stays the same, P (prices) must double. You haven't made anyone richer. You've just made every mark worth half as much... and then people figure that out.

What Happened Next — Breaking the Spiral
How Hyperinflation Ends
Germany Killed the Mark and Started Over
The Rentenmark Fix
In November 1923, Germany replaced the mark entirely. 1 Rentenmark = 1 trillion old marks. A hard cap was placed on how many could ever be printed, ending the open-ended money creation that had caused the crisis.
Why It Actually Worked
The Rentenmark wasn't backed by gold. It was backed by a credible promise not to keep printing. People believed it would hold its value, expectations flipped, panic spending stopped, and velocity fell back to normal. The doom loop ran in reverse.
Key Takeaway
The Rentenmark worked not because of what it was made of, but because people believed in it. This is the whole foundation of fiat money: money has value because we collectively agree it does. Destroy that agreement and no amount of printing can save it.
Connecting to the Bigger Picture
Preview · Monetary Policy
Why Doesn't This Happen in the USA?
Two words: the Fed. The United States has an independent central bank whose whole job is to manage the money supply so we never end up in a Weimar situation. Germany in 1923 had no such institution. The government printed money whenever it needed to pay bills, with no one empowered to say no. The Federal Reserve can raise interest rates and slow the money supply even when the government doesn't want it to, and it has decades of credibility built up doing exactly that. When the Fed says it will keep inflation near 2%, people believe it, and as Weimar showed us, belief is literally everything. A central bank without credibility is just a printing press with a fancier name.
